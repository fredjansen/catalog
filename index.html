Introduction

The Practitioner-Researcher Chasm

The field of software engineering is characterized by a fundamental dichotomy in the creation and dissemination of knowledge. On one side exists the formal, rigorously peer-reviewed Academic Literature (AL), primarily produced by researchers and published in journals and conference proceedings controlled by academic publishers. On the other side lies a vast and dynamic body of work known as Grey Literature (GL), which is predominantly produced by practitioners—the software engineers, architects, and technical leaders working within the industry.

A critical observation is that these two communities, researchers and practitioners, largely operate within distinct informational ecosystems. They tend to read and contribute to literature produced almost exclusively by and for their own community. This separation is not incidental; it is a structural reality of the field.

Key Barriers: Practitioners rarely publish in or consume content from academic forums. The barriers are significant: academic journals are often locked behind expensive paywalls, the prose can be dense and theoretical, and the time required to parse a scientific paper is a luxury few working professionals can afford.

The proliferation of software engineering grey literature can be understood as a direct, functional response to this structural gap. The professional demands on software practitioners necessitate a constant intake of current, practical, and context-specific information to solve immediate, real-world problems. When the traditional academic publishing model fails to meet this demand for timely and accessible knowledge, a vacuum is created.

Practitioners have filled this vacuum by establishing their own vibrant ecosystem of content creation and distribution channels—including blogs, forums, and technical reports—which are inherently more agile, accessible, and aligned with their needs. Therefore, the existence of this GL ecosystem is not a peripheral phenomenon but a necessary, purpose-built infrastructure for knowledge exchange.

Formalizing the Definition of Grey Literature

While the concept of grey literature has existed for decades, its definition has been refined over time to accommodate the digital age. The most widely accepted and frequently cited formal definition is the "Luxembourg definition," established in 1997 and expanded in 2004.

Luxembourg Definition: Grey literature is "that which is produced on all levels of government, academics, business and industry in print and electronic formats, but which is not controlled by commercial publishers." The key criterion here is the lack of control by commercial publishing entities, whose primary business is publication.

In the specific context of software engineering, this formal definition is often supplemented by simpler, more functional characterizations. The most common of these defines GL as any literature that has not been subjected to a formal, independent peer-review process before publication.

Under these definitions, the umbrella of grey literature is exceptionally broad. It encompasses a diverse range of document types produced by a variety of actors:

  • Reports: Technical reports, progress reports, market research reports, government reports, white papers
  • Academic and Pre-Publication Materials: Theses and dissertations (ETDs), preprints, e-prints, working papers
  • Corporate and Organizational Documents: Technical specifications and standards, manuals, product data
  • Conference Materials: Conference proceedings, posters, presentations, and slide decks
  • Online and Community-Generated Content: Blog posts, discussion forum threads, Q&A site content, newsletters, wikis
  • Audiovisual Media: Online videos, tutorials, recorded presentations, and podcasts

Features Analysis Framework

This research employs a comprehensive framework for analyzing grey literature sources in software engineering, organized into five main feature categories with detailed characteristics for systematic evaluation.

1. Basic Features

Fundamental characteristics that define each grey literature source:

  • Associated Site: Platform affiliation (reddit, stack exchange, microsoft, quora)
  • Creation Date: Year the site was established
  • Website Organization: Structure (topics, areas, communities)
  • Registration Methods: Available login options
  • Search Engines: Search capabilities (string, tags, similarity)
  • Type of Audience: Target users (developers, engineers, architects, testers)
  • Website Type: Classification (forum, community, blog, website)
  • Subarea of Software Engineering: Focus areas (general, coding, architecture, maintenance, testing, management, evolution)

2. Connection and Data Features

Technical capabilities and data accessibility:

  • Has its own API: API availability for data access
  • Can download repository: Repository download capabilities
  • Has its own Dashboard: Dashboard availability
  • Has its app: Mobile/desktop application availability

3. Statistical Features

Quantitative metrics and engagement data:

  • Daily Audience: Average daily users
  • Total Users Registered: Total registered user count
  • Question Rated: Question rating systems
  • Answer Rated: Answer rating mechanisms
  • Percentual Answered: Question answer rates
  • Average Interactions per Question: Engagement metrics
  • Total of Citations: Academic citations and references

4. On Board Features

User participation and community management:

  • Moderation Type: Content moderation approaches
  • Monetization Type: Revenue models and monetization strategies
  • Basic Required Information: Registration requirements
  • Needed Qualification: User qualification requirements
  • Users Ranking: User ranking and reputation systems
  • Actions Allowed: Permitted user actions
  • Repository Metadata: Repository description availability

5. Discussion Features

Communication patterns and content characteristics:

  • Most Quoted Kind of Topics: Predominant discussion themes
  • Experience Level: User experience distribution
  • Tone of Discussions: Communication style (collaborative, competitive, informal)
  • Quality Level of Answers: Answer quality assessment
  • Non-technical User Profile: User diversity characteristics
  • Most Quoted Languages: Programming languages discussed
  • Most Quoted Frameworks: Popular frameworks and technologies

Research Methodology: Each feature is systematically evaluated using predefined criteria with expected values to ensure consistency and comparability across all 41 grey literature sources in the catalog.

Research Limitations

This research acknowledges several limitations that should be considered when interpreting and applying the findings from this grey literature catalog.

Scope and Coverage Limitations

Source Selection Constraints

The catalog includes 41 sources, which represents a significant but not exhaustive sample of grey literature in software engineering. The selection criteria focused on English-language sources with substantial user bases and active communities.

  • Language bias toward English-language sources
  • Geographic representation may favor Western platforms
  • Emerging or niche platforms may be underrepresented
  • Platform availability and accessibility at time of research

Temporal Limitations

Grey literature sources are dynamic and evolve continuously. The analysis represents a snapshot of characteristics at the time of data collection.

  • Platform features and policies change over time
  • User demographics and behavior patterns evolve
  • New platforms emerge while others become obsolete
  • Statistical data reflects point-in-time measurements

Methodological Limitations

Data Collection Constraints

The automated analysis approach, while systematic, has inherent limitations in capturing nuanced characteristics of complex socio-technical systems.

  • Reliance on publicly available information
  • Limited access to internal platform analytics
  • Potential discrepancies in self-reported statistics
  • Subjective interpretation of qualitative characteristics

Classification Challenges

Categorizing grey literature sources involves subjective decisions, particularly for platforms that serve multiple purposes or audiences.

  • Overlapping categories and hybrid platforms
  • Evolution of platform purposes over time
  • Multiple audience segments within single platforms
  • Varying interpretation of feature definitions

Future Research Directions: Future research should consider longitudinal studies, broader geographic coverage, multilingual analysis, and integration with quantitative usage analytics to address these limitations and enhance understanding of grey literature ecosystems.
